Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (India) -- Summary of Key Provisions
Sources: Official Act text (meity.gov.in), Ministry of Electronics and Information
Technology. This is an original summary of publicly available legal provisions,
not a copy of any third-party article.

Official Preamble (direct from the Act, Government of India, No. 22 of 2023, assented 11 August 2023):

"An Act to provide for the processing of digital personal data in a manner that
recognises both the right of individuals to protect their personal data and the
need to process such personal data for lawful purposes and for matters connected
therewith or incidental thereto."

Background

The DPDP Act, 2023 is India's first comprehensive law governing digital personal
data protection. It followed the Supreme Court's 2017 ruling in Justice K.S.
Puttaswamy vs. Union of India, which declared privacy a fundamental right under
Article 21 of the Constitution. The Act replaces earlier, narrower rules under the
Information Technology Act, 2000 and the IT (Reasonable Security Practices) Rules,
2011. Companion Rules (the DPDP Rules) were notified in 2025 with an 18-month
phased compliance timeline for organisations.

Key Terms

- Data Principal: the individual to whom the personal data relates.
- Data Fiduciary: any person or organisation that determines the purpose and
  means of processing personal data (e.g. a business collecting customer data).
- Data Processor: an entity that processes data on behalf of a Data Fiduciary.
- Personal Data Breach: any unauthorised processing, or accidental disclosure,
  acquisition, or loss of access to personal data.

Seven Core Principles the Act Is Built On

1. Consent and transparency
2. Purpose limitation (data used only for the purpose it was collected for)
3. Data minimisation (collect only what is necessary)
4. Accuracy of data
5. Storage limitation (data not retained longer than necessary)
6. Reasonable security safeguards
7. Accountability of the Data Fiduciary

Consent Requirements

- Data Fiduciaries must issue clear, standalone consent notices, in plain
  language, explaining the specific purpose of data collection.
- Consent must be freely given, specific, informed, unconditional, and
  unambiguous, with a clear affirmative action.
- Verifiable parental/guardian consent is required before processing a child's
  (under 18) personal data, with limited exemptions for purposes like
  healthcare, education, and real-time safety monitoring.
- A Data Principal can withdraw consent at any time, as easily as it was given.

Rights of Data Principals

- Right to access information about their personal data being processed.
- Right to correction, updating, and erasure of personal data.
- Right to nominate another individual to exercise these rights on their
  behalf (e.g. in case of death or incapacity).
- Right to grievance redressal.
- Data Fiduciaries must respond to such requests within a specified timeline
  (up to 90 days depending on the request type).

Obligations of Data Fiduciaries

- Implement reasonable security safeguards to prevent personal data breaches.
- In the event of a breach, promptly notify both the affected Data Principals
  and the Data Protection Board of India, in plain language describing the
  nature and likely consequences of the breach and the steps being taken.
- Ensure data accuracy and completeness where the data is used to make a
  decision affecting the Data Principal, or is likely to be shared with
  another Data Fiduciary.
- Delete personal data once the purpose for processing is no longer being
  served, unless retention is required by law (storage limitation).
- Cross-border data transfer is permitted by default, except to countries
  specifically restricted by the Central Government via notification (this is
  a notably more liberal approach than the earlier 2018/2019 draft bills,
  which proposed stricter data-localisation requirements).

Enforcement

- The Data Protection Board of India is the enforcement body, functioning as
  a digital-first institution where complaints can be filed and tracked
  online.
- Penalties for non-compliance can be substantial (up to hundreds of crores of
  rupees for serious violations such as failure to prevent a data breach or
  failure to notify affected individuals).

Why this matters for a chat-commerce order-risk platform

- Any platform that stores buyer phone numbers, addresses, and order/delivery
  history for risk scoring counts as a Data Fiduciary under this Act.
- Consent must be collected before storing and using buyer data, including for
  a cross-seller "buyer trust network" style feature.
- Data must be minimised, accurate, and deleted once no longer needed --
  relevant to how long raw chat logs or address history should be retained.
- Any data breach involving buyer phone numbers or addresses would trigger a
  mandatory notification obligation.
